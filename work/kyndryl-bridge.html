01

Brief

Kyndryl (NYSE: KD) is the world's largest IT-infrastructure services provider, serving 75 of the Fortune 100. Bridge is its open-integration PaaS — a single console for AI, AIOps, and automation across customers' multi-cloud estates.

The product had to speak to CIOs operating at maximum complexity while staying actionable for the operations engineers using it every day. One platform. Two audiences. Zero tolerance for friction.

02

My role

Associate Director, UX Design. I led the strategy-to-ship arc: research direction, information architecture for the unified console, design-system integration, testing with Maze, and stakeholder alignment across product and engineering.

The seat was senior IC and people leader at once — strategy in the room, hands on the work.

03

The challenge

Enterprise complexity at maximum scale. 190+ services to surface coherently under one mental model. Multi-cloud environments where every customer's setup was different. A CIO audience with zero patience for the third click. And a global engineering org that had been shipping services faster than the UX could rationalise them.

The challenge wasn't pretty screens. It was a build-able strategy.

04

What I drove

Research strategy and execution across customer segments. Information architecture that took 190+ services and gave them one mental model. Design-system integration so the platform aged well as services were added. Usability testing with Maze, run by my team. Stakeholder alignment — the quiet work — across product, engineering, and customer-success teams.

Workshops on the wall. AI on the desk. Pencil and paper when the whiteboarding ran long.

05

The outcome

  • →500+ global enterprise customers operational on the platform
  • →$2 billion in annual productivity savings delivered
  • →190+ services launched — past the company's own target
  • →84% reduction in time to resolution
  • →70% reduction in incident volume
  • →25% improvement in cloud resource costs

The numbers are the proof. The shift is the point: from a product that explained its services to a product that resolved its customers' incidents.

06

What I'd do differently

I'd push earlier and harder for a single source of truth on customer journeys. We had multiple research streams running in parallel, and synthesis happened later than it should have. A central journey map, maintained as a living artefact from week one, would have accelerated every decision downstream by months.

The lesson: in a product this broad, the artefact that aligns everyone has to be made before it's asked for.

07

How I worked

Diagrams before decks. Workshops on the wall. Synthesis in Claude and Dovetail. Prototypes in Figma with AI on the side. The discipline didn't change with the tools — the velocity did. Twenty years of practice compressed into half the calendar time. That's the unlock AI gives a senior designer who already knows what good looks like.

The best design work is invisible to the user.
