01 · The Brief

A bank branch without walls

Concept · Finance · Spatial UX

A small finance bank operating in underserved urban and peri-urban markets needed to extend its reach beyond its physical branch footprint — without the capital cost of new branches. The brief: explore whether a virtual branch experience, delivered via a consumer VR headset, could authentically replicate the trust, completeness, and compliance rigour of an in-branch banking session.

The challenge was not purely technological. It was a design problem: can a first-time VR user complete complex, high-stakes financial transactions — on their own, without a bank officer — inside a three-dimensional spatial environment?

  • →End-to-end from requirements gathering through to functioning prototype on device
  • →Compliance-grade authentication — Aadhaar biometric and secondary OTP verification
  • →Five production flows: balance check, FD creation, FD renewal, RD creation, RD renewal
  • →User testing with participants who had zero prior VR experience
  • →A reusable spatial UX methodology — not a one-off prototype

02 · Discovery

Requirements gathering from first principles

Research · Stakeholder Alignment

There is no prior playbook for VR banking in the Indian context. No NNG report to reference. No Nielsen heuristics calibrated for spatial financial UX. Every requirement had to be surfaced through structured discovery — from banking operations experts, compliance teams, and prospective end users.

Discovery ran across three tracks simultaneously: regulatory & compliance (what authentication does RBI mandate for banking transactions?), banking operations (what does a teller actually do, and can a VR interface replicate that sequence?), and human factors (what spatial constraints does a first-time VR user face that a 2D screen user never encounters?).

  • →Stakeholder interviews with bank operations, compliance, and technology leadership
  • →Task analysis of physical branch workflows — decomposed to discrete interaction steps
  • →Regulatory audit — RBI guidelines on digital KYC, Aadhaar-based authentication, OTP standards
  • →Competitive audit of global VR banking prototypes and spatial finance concepts
  • →Persona development — three target user profiles, varying digital literacy, age, and VR exposure

The single largest finding from discovery: banking is a trust transaction, not just a task transaction. Any VR experience that prioritised interaction efficiency over perceived safety would fail at the compliance and user confidence level simultaneously.

03 · Figma

Figma workflows calibrated to the headset

Prototyping · Spatial UI

Figma does not support native VR prototyping — but it is still the right tool for spatial UI design when used correctly. The key shift: every frame was designed at the correct angular subtense for a VR viewport (roughly 100° FoV), with UI elements placed at simulated arm-reach depth using scale as a proxy for z-distance.

The prototyping approach developed a three-layer system: environment shell (the spatial room/context), information panels (floating UI surfaces), and interaction hotspots (the gaze-dwell or controller-trigger targets). Each layer was designed independently, then composed into full-scene views that could be walked through in Figma's presentation mode as a spatial storyboard.

  • 01Environment architecture — spatial room geometry defined in wireframe before any UI was placed
  • 02VR-calibrated component library — minimum 48dp targets, no text below 18pt at 1m simulated depth
  • 03Panel system — 3 spatial panel types: primary action, status/confirmation, ambient context
  • 04Figma prototype flows mimicking actual headset navigation — gaze targets, controller maps, back gestures
  • 05100% of all banking flows prototyped in Figma before a single line of device code was written

04 · Auth Design

Aadhaar & double authentication in VR

Security · Compliance · Trust

Authentication in a VR headset is the hardest unsolved problem in spatial banking UX. The user cannot see their phone clearly while wearing the headset. Fingerprint readers don't exist inside the virtual environment. OTP fields require typed input in a medium where typing is non-trivially difficult for first-time users.

The authentication architecture required a new approach: a two-stage pass-through model. Stage one uses Aadhaar-based biometric verification at entry — the user's Aadhaar-linked mobile OTP is entered via a large-format VR numpad designed for minimal errors, with immediate haptic feedback on every input. Stage two is a secondary PIN or face-match confirmation gate before each transaction. Both stages are fully compliant with RBI's digital KYC mandates for high-value transactions.

The key insight: trust is designed, not assumed. Every authentication step needed visual confirmation language that explicitly told the user what had just happened and what comes next — a pattern that 2D banking apps routinely skip because the screen metaphor carries implied trust.

  • →Aadhaar OTP entry via large-target VR numpad (60dp minimum, glanceable error feedback)
  • →Transaction-level secondary PIN gate — visible progress indicator for each auth stage
  • →Spatial confirmation language — full-screen confirmation panels, not toast notifications
  • →Session timeout with graceful spatial exit — no abrupt black screen, always explained to user
  • →RBI-compliant audit trail embedded at the UX level — every step surfaced, not buried in logs

05 · Device

Bringing it to life on Meta Quest 3

Meta Quest 3 · Spatial Build

The Meta Quest 3 was chosen over the Quest Pro and PSVR2 for one reason: it is the device closest to what a small finance bank could realistically deploy at branch kiosks or lend to customers — sub-$600, standalone, no PC tethering, no console dependency. The design had to work within its real constraints, not a best-case hardware scenario.

The spatial build translated every Figma frame into a realised three-dimensional environment. The virtual bank branch includes a reception lobby (the entry and orientation space), three service counters (Savings, Deposits, Enquiry), and a transaction confirmation chamber — a distinct spatial zone used only for completed transactions, giving the user an unmistakable signal that something real just happened.

  • 01Quest 3 standalone build — no PC dependency, offline-capable for demo contexts
  • 02Controller-mapped interaction model — trigger for select, thumbstick for navigation, B/Y for back
  • 03Mixed reality mode tested — virtual branch layered into physical environment for context orientation
  • 04Performance tuning — consistent 72fps achieved across all flows, no frame drop during auth panels
  • 05Accessibility settings — comfort mode for locomotion-sensitive users, font scale controls

06 · User Testing

Tested with people who had never worn a headset

Research · Usability · Validation

The test population was deliberately chosen for maximum naivety with respect to VR: participants ranged from 24 to 58 years of age, with no prior VR headset experience. Several had limited digital banking experience. This was not convenience sampling — it was an explicit design decision to validate that the product worked at the edge of the intended audience, not the comfortable middle.

Testing was moderated, with think-aloud protocol, and each session was observed by two researchers. Participants completed all five banking flows in a single session without any task-specific coaching after the initial orientation. The orientation itself was measured and scripted — a deliberate constraint to keep it deployable in an unmanned kiosk context.

  • →Participant profiles: first-time VR users across three age bands and two digital literacy levels
  • →Moderated think-aloud sessions — all five flows completed in a single unbroken session
  • →Scripted orientation — same 90-second intro every participant received, no deviation
  • →Biometric signals tracked: head movement patterns, dwell time on UI elements, controller hesitation
  • →Post-session SUS (System Usability Scale) and exit interview
  • →All sessions video-recorded — screen-in-headset capture + external observation camera

The most important usability finding: spatial orientation, not interaction complexity, was the primary source of user anxiety. Users who understood where they were in the virtual space completed transactions with confidence. Users who felt spatially lost abandoned tasks even when they understood the UI perfectly.

07 · The Flows

Five banking transactions, fully realised

Interaction Design · Task Flows

Each of the five flows was designed to be completable end-to-end — not a demo stub, not a happy-path only, but a production-grade interaction sequence including error states, loading states, confirmation flows, and graceful recovery from authentication failure.

The simplest flow, and the first one every test participant completed. Balance check established the spatial mental model: counter navigation → service selection → authentication gate → information panel → return to lobby. This sequence repeated (with variation) across all subsequent flows.

08 · Findings

What the testing actually revealed

Research Synthesis

User testing with naive participants produced a richer finding set than any internal design review could have generated. The strongest patterns cut across all five participant profiles regardless of age or digital literacy.

Finding 01 — Validated

All five flows completable

Every participant, without exception, completed all five banking transactions in a single session. Completion time decreased sharply after the first flow — by FD creation, all participants were significantly faster than their balance-check baseline.

Finding 02 — Spatial

Location = confidence

Participants who could identify their spatial position within the virtual branch (lobby vs. counter vs. confirmation zone) expressed significantly higher confidence in transaction accuracy, regardless of whether they were correct about what the UI was doing.

Finding 03 — Auth

OTP was the most stressful moment

Aadhaar OTP entry — despite the large-format numpad — was the highest-anxiety moment in every session. Not because the UI was difficult, but because participants were aware a real financial credential was being entered. This is a trust signal, not a usability failure.

Finding 04 — Design

Confirmation chamber was the hit

The spatially distinct transaction completion zone — the "confirmation chamber" — was spontaneously mentioned by 4 of 5 participants as making them feel the transaction was "real and done." The spatial metaphor of entering a different room for completion worked.

Finding 05 — Iteration

Text legibility at depth

FD rate projection panels, designed at 18pt at 1m depth, were on the edge of legibility for participants over 45. A post-test design fix scaled these to 22pt minimum. This was a prototyping constraint that did not exist in the Figma simulation.

Finding 06 — Methodology

Figma-first worked

Every Figma prototype flow predicted the real-device interaction sequence within one decision-branch. Zero flows required structural redesign after device testing. The spatial storyboarding approach in Figma proved valid as a VR prototyping methodology.

09 · Outcomes

What this project established

Impact · Methodology

The VR branch banking project produced three distinct outputs: a working prototype, a validated user testing dataset, and a reusable spatial UX methodology. The methodology is arguably the most durable — it is a documented process for designing banking-grade spatial experiences that can be applied to future headset generations and adjacent domains.

  • →First fully user-tested VR banking prototype with Aadhaar-compliant authentication in the Indian market
  • →All five production banking flows validated with first-time VR users — no prior headset experience required
  • →Spatial UX methodology documented and transferable — applicable to AR/XR products beyond banking
  • →Figma-to-device prototyping process established as a valid VR design workflow — no purpose-built VR tools required
  • →Confirmation chamber design pattern adopted as a standard for high-stakes transaction UX in spatial environments
  • →Client used prototype as the centrepiece of their innovation presentation to board and regulator stakeholders
10 · Reflection

What I'd do differently

Critique · Growth

The Figma prototype was designed for a moderator-present testing context. The scripted 90-second orientation was sufficient for a facilitated session — but would not survive an unmanned kiosk deployment without a significantly more robust onboarding flow. I'd invest more time here: a self-paced spatial tutorial that teaches headset navigation through a low-stakes task before any financial flow begins.

The authentication UX solved the compliance problem — but the emotional experience of entering financial credentials inside a headset remains a design open question. I'd run a dedicated auth-only study before committing to the numpad pattern, exploring voice input and face authentication as alternatives that feel less intrusive in a fully immersive environment.

  • →Invest in a self-paced spatial onboarding tutorial — not just a scripted orientation
  • →Run an auth-only usability study to explore voice and face alternatives to numpad OTP
  • →Design for amblyopia and vestibular sensitivity from day one — not as an accessibility afterthought
  • →Build a design system, not just a prototype — so the next VR product in this domain starts faster

The best spatial design makes you forget the headset.
